> Define mineral carbonation and explain the chemical mechanism behind its ability to sequester CO2 safely.

**Answer:**
> Mineral carbonation is an advanced carbon capture and storage (CCS) technique that involves the chemical reaction of carbon dioxide (CO2) with alkaline earth metal oxides or hydroxides—specifically those containing calcium (Ca) or magnesium (Mg)—to form stable, solid carbonate materials. The fundamental mechanism relies on a thermodynamic reaction where CO2 reacts with these divalent metal oxides to produce stable minerals such as calcite (CaCO3), dolomite (Ca0.5Mg0.5CO3), magnesite (MgCO3), or siderite (FeCO3). Because the resulting carbonate products are thermodynamically stable and exist in a solid, mineralized form, this method ensures permanent and safe sequestration, effectively preventing the CO2 from being released back into the atmosphere. The reaction itself is exothermic, meaning it releases heat, which provides a theoretical advantage where the process energy could potentially be harvested for power generation. However, the primary challenge in this mechanism is accelerating the reaction rate to be commercially viable while managing the heat release and ensuring the environmental safety of the surrounding geological site.
